Chapter 01

The first thing you touch

01 · The Wake-Up
32%
check their phone within five minutes of waking

Before coffee. Before words. The first conscious act of the day, for nearly one in three British smartphone owners, is reaching for the glass rectangle on their bedside table.

01 · The First Act
News is what they're after — first

27% head straight to news and current affairs. The smartphone has become the nation's unofficial morning paper, consumed in bed before the kettle has even boiled.

01 · The Social Loop
Then: the social reflex kicks in

Social media (17%) and personal messaging (16%) follow close behind. Three of the top four morning activities are about connecting with other people — however passively.

01 · Work Creep
Work has no morning boundary

9% check work email before they've left the house. The separation between life and work is dissolving — one early-morning notification at a time.

Chapter 02

The device that never sleeps

02 · The Commute
78%
are using their phones on public transport by 8am

The morning commute has been colonised. Three quarters of smartphone owners are scrolling through feeds while the city passes outside the window.

02 · The Dinner Table
68%
use their phone during family dinner

The family dinner table used to be a sanctuary from the outside world. No longer. The phone has quietly claimed a place setting of its own.

02 · The Second Screen
81%
scroll while the television plays

By 9pm, four in five smartphone owners are using their phone while watching TV. The television alone can no longer command full attention.

02 · 3 in the Morning
34%
check the phone in the middle of the night

For one in three, the day doesn't end at sleep. They wake at 3am and, almost reflexively, reach for the phone. Even rest is interrupted.

Chapter 03

The device that overtook everything

03 · The Ranking
Every portable device, ranked by ownership

From laptops to VR headsets, British adults carry a dizzying array of portable technology in 2016. But one device has quietly moved to the very front of the queue.

03 · The Overtake
81%
Smartphone takes the crown — for the first time

The smartphone has surpassed the laptop as Britain's most commonly owned portable device. A technology that did not exist in its current form until 2007 now sits atop the hierarchy.

03 · The Tail
Everything else lags far behind

The gap to the rest is dramatic. Smart watches sit at 4%, VR headsets at 3%. These remain curiosities at the margins, while the phone has become essential infrastructure.

Chapter 04

The end of the growth era

04 · The Sprint
Penetration grew at a furious pace

In 2013, smartphone ownership grew by nineteen percentage points in a single year. The technology was sweeping through Britain — from early adopters to the mainstream, the hesitant, the converted.

04 · The Deceleration
The sprint became a jog

By 2015, annual growth had fallen to 9 percentage points. The low-hanging fruit had been picked. Most people who were going to adopt had adopted.

04 · The Reckoning
+7pp
2016: Deloitte calls it "the end of the growth era"

Just seven percentage points of new penetration in 2016. The smartphone has reached a saturation point. The race for ownership is effectively over.

Chapter 05

The body as password

05 · The Fingerprint Age
21%
use their fingerprint to unlock their phone

A fifth of all British smartphone owners have enrolled their fingerprint. Biometric authentication — once the preserve of spy films — has landed in the average pocket.

05 · The Dominance
Fingerprint dwarfs all alternatives

Voice recognition and facial recognition sit at just 2% each. The fingerprint hasn't just won the biometric race — it lapped the competition before they found the starting line.

05 · The Adoption Rate
76%
of fingerprint reader owners have activated it

64% say their phone has a fingerprint reader. Of those, three quarters have switched it on. The barrier is not willingness — it is hardware availability.

"Perhaps the more honest question is not whether we are addicted to our phones, but whether that framing still matters — when the phone has already become infrastructure."
